Aloevera Rot

FOUND THIS IMAGE IS ALOEVERA ROT AFFECTED


Prevention

Maintain well-draining soil to prevent water accumulation around the roots. Allow the soil to dry between watering sessions, ensuring proper ventilation. Avoid planting Aloe vera in containers without drainage holes.


Precaution

Immediately remove any rot-affected leaves and inspect the roots for signs of decay. Adjust watering frequency and volume, especially during cooler seasons when the plant requires less moisture.Additional Precaution: Ensure adequate sunlight and avoid overcrowding to promote air circulation, reducing the risk of fungal infections. Use a balanced fertilizer sparingly, as over-fertilization can weaken the plant and make it more susceptible to rot.


Causes

Aloe vera rot is primarily caused by overwatering, leading to waterlogged soil and root suffocation. Poor drainage and excessive moisture create favorable conditions for fungal and bacterial pathogens, contributing to rot.

Aloevera Rust

FOUND THIS IMAGE IS ALOEVERA RUST AFFECTED


Prevention

Maintain a well-ventilated environment by providing adequate spacing between plants. Water the soil directly and avoid wetting the foliage to minimize conditions conducive to rust development. Ensure proper sunlight exposure to inhibit fungal growth.


Precaution

Promptly remove rust-affected leaves to prevent the spread of spores. Consider applying organic fungicides like neem oil to inhibit fungal growth. Regularly inspect plants for early signs of rust and treat them immediately. Additional Precaution: Quarantine new Aloe vera plants before introducing them to the garden to prevent potential rust contamination. Keep the growing area clean and free from debris to minimize the risk of fungal spore accumulation.


Causes

Aloe vera rust is caused by a fungal infection, typically Puccinia aloes, favored by high humidity and inadequate air circulation. Spores spread through water or wind, leading to orange-brown rust-like spots on leaves.

Basil Mildew

FOUND THIS IMAGE IS BASIL MILDEW AFFECTED


Prevention

Ensure well-draining soil, proper spacing, and adequate air circulation to reduce humidity around basil plants. Water at the base to keep foliage dry and avoid splashing water on leaves, preventing favorable conditions for mildew.


Precaution

Regularly inspect basil for early signs of mildew, such as yellow spots or fuzzy growth on the undersides of leaves. Immediately remove affected leaves, and consider applying organic fungicides like neem oil preventatively.Additional Precaution: Plant disease-resistant basil varieties and rotate crops yearly to minimize the risk of persistent mildew. Quarantine new basil plants before introducing them to the garden to prevent the spread of potential infections.


Causes

Basil mildew is caused by fungal infections like downy mildew (Peronospora belbahrii), thriving in humid conditions. Spores spread through water, air, or contaminated tools, affecting basil leaves with characteristic yellowing and mold-like growth.

Cardamom Leaf Blight

FOUND THIS IMAGE IS CARDAMOM LEAF BLIGHT AFFECTED


Prevention

Practice strict sanitation measures, including cleaning and disinfecting tools, and using disease-free seeds. Ensure proper spacing and pruning for optimal air circulation, reducing humidity. Apply copper-based fungicides preventatively.


Precaution

Regularly inspect cardamom plants for early signs of leaf blight, such as brown lesions and leaf discoloration. Immediately remove and destroy infected plant parts to prevent further spread. Quarantine new plants before introduction to the plantation.Additional Precaution: Enhance plant vigor through balanced fertilization and watering practices, promoting resistance to leaf blight. Rotate crops and avoid continuous monoculture to disrupt the disease cycle and minimize the risk of recurrent infections.


Causes

Cardamom leaf blight is caused by the fungus Pestalotiopsis spp., spreading through infected seeds or contaminated tools. High humidity, poor air circulation, and dense canopy contribute to its rapid spread, leading to brown lesions on cardamom leaves.

FOUND THIS IMAGE IS CARDAMOM LEAF SPOT AFFECTED

Cassava Bacterial Blight

FOUND THIS IMAGE IS CASSAVA BACTERIAL BLIGHT AFFECTED


Prevention

Use disease-free planting material and practice strict sanitation, including tool disinfection. Maintain proper spacing between cassava plants to enhance air circulation, reducing humidity. Plant resistant varieties to minimize susceptibility.


Precaution

Regularly inspect cassava plants for symptoms such as leaf lesions and wilting. Remove and destroy infected plant parts, and avoid working in wet fields to prevent bacterial dissemination. Causes

Cassava bacterial blight is caused by the bacterium Xanthomonas axonopodis, primarily transmitted through infected planting material, contaminated tools, or rain splash. Warm and humid conditions facilitate bacterial spread, resulting in angular leaf lesions and stem cankers.
